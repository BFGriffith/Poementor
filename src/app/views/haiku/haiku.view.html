Haiku 俳句 (plural also “haiku”) is a short form style of Japanese poem. Haiku written in Japanese are typically printed in a single vertical line, whereas English language haiku usually have three lines to parallel the three phrases of Japanese haiku. Haiku is a style of poetry that traditionally focuses on “cutting” (kuru 切る) by juxtaposing two images or ideas with a kereji (“cutting word”) in between them which signals the separation, and may indicate or underscore how the juxtaposed elements are related.

  • Traditional Japanese language haiku consist of 17 morae (often roughly translated as “syllables”), and written as three phrases of 5, 7, and 5.
  • Most, though not all, haiku involve nature; traditional haiku contain a “kigo” (“seasonal reference”) drawn from a saijiki, which is an extensive list of such words.
  • Even some modern haiku that buck tradition and don’t exactly follow the traditional structure or take nature as a major part of their subject still almost always tend to use juxtaposition, sometimes of directly observed everyday objects or occurrences.
  • Previously called hokku, haiku was given its current name by the Japanese writer Masaoka Shiki at the end of the 19th century
